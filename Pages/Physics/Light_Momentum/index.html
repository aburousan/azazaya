Introduction

While studying many topics we have used that fact that, the momentum of light is given by,

p=Ec p = \frac{E}{c} p=cE​

In this eqn-(1), EEE represent the energy of light and ccc is the speed of light.

Normally, the proof is given by using Einstein Mass-Energy Equivalence formula. The proof is as follows:

Proof:

E2=(mc2)2+(pc)2 E^2 = (mc^2)^2 + (pc)^2 E2=(mc2)2+(pc)2

Now, as light doesn't have any mass, we put m=0m=0m=0, which gives us our expression for the momentum ppp.

The problem with this proof is that fact It doesn't teach us what is physically happening nor it tells us how the hell something which doesn't have any mass can have momentum?

What is Momentum?

Normally, we say Momentum is a measurement of mass in motion: how much mass is in how much motion. It is usually given the symbol p⃗\vec{p}p​ and it is given by p⃗=mv⃗\vec{p} = m\vec{v}p​=mv. But this is not correct in general.

In many streams of physics, like Analytical Mechanics or Special Theory of Relativity, we have some different expression for momentum. Those expressions boils down to this one in some special case, i.e., p⃗=mv⃗\vec{p} = m\vec{v}p​=mv is not true in general. Then, what exactly is momentum?

Momentum is a property of any object which tells us it's ability to push or apply force to some other object.

This means even if some obejct don't have any mass if it can push something we can conclude that it has some momentum (just remember F⃗=dp⃗dt\vec{F} = \frac{d \vec{p}}{dt}F=dtdp​​). Maybe we will explore this point in much more detail in some future blog.

Origin of Momentum of Light

We know Light is a Electro-Magnetic wave. The electric and magnetic field oscillate perpendicular to each other and propagate in the perpendicular direction as shown below.

The blue one is electric field and red one is magnetic field.

What happens for the case of Magnetic Field?
In this case again let's take an electron at some point. The magnetic field is pointing in the −x^-\hat{x}−x^ direction and due to the electric field the electron will have a velocity in the +z^+\hat{z}+z^ direction.

Using the Lorentz Force equation,

F⃗m=q(v⃗×B⃗) \vec{F}_m = q(\vec{v}\times \vec{B}) Fm​=q(v×B)

where qqq is the charge of the particle, v⃗\vec{v}v is the velocity and B⃗\vec{B}B is the magnetic field, we see that the magnetic field will push the electron in the +y^+\hat{y}+y^​ direction with a value of evBevBevB (Note: electron has negative charge).

After a while when the magnetic field is now pointing in the +x^+\hat{x}+x^ direction, the velocity of the electron will be along the −z^-\hat{z}−z^ direction. Here applying the Lorentz Force formula again, will show us that the electron will be pushed in the +y^+\hat{y}+y^​ direction once again.

Hence, there is a Net non-zero push, i.e., There is a net momentum transfer.

This shows us that Light's momentum is actually provided by the Magnetic Field. So, It is the magnetic field who gives light it's momentum. But if it's true we should be able to derive the eqn-(1). Let's try to derive that.

Proof: The magnetic force on the electron on both case is,

F⃗m=evBy^→Fm=evB \vec{F}_m = evB \hat{y} \to F_m = evB Fm​=evBy^​→Fm​=evB

Now, we know that BBB and EEE follow the relation,

EB=c \frac{E}{B} = c BE​=c

Fm=evEc=vFec F_m = \frac{evE}{c} = \frac{vF_e}{c} Fm​=cevE​=cvFe​​

where Fe=eEF_e = eEFe​=eE is the electric force on the electron.

Note: This tells us that as v<cv< cv<c, Electric Force will always be greater than Magnetic force.
